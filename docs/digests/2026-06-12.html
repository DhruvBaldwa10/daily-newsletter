AI Budgets Break, PRs Fail

Engineering orgs are quietly pulling back on AI spend even as agents proliferate. New research finds nearly half of AI-generated pull requests get rejected. And Google DeepMind just put $10M on the table to figure out why multi-agent systems keep misbehaving.

The AI Spend Reckoning Engineering Teams Didn't Expect

Two months ago it was token budgets exploding as agent usage surged across engineering orgs. This week, Gergely Orosz reports a counter-trend forming: companies are actively trying to cut back on AI spend within engineering departments. The context matters — the same newsletter documented "tokenmaxxing" in April, where engineers at Meta, Microsoft, and Salesforce were deliberately burning tokens to hit AI usage metrics. Now the pendulum is swinging. Finance teams saw the bills, and the conversation has changed from "how do we scale AI adoption" to "how do we justify what we're already spending."

This is the maturity curve playing out in real time. The pilot-to-production gap isn't just technical — it's financial. Orgs that rolled out Copilot enterprise licenses, Claude Code seats, and Cursor subscriptions simultaneously are now doing the math on cost-per-merged-PR. When you layer in the rejection rates (more on that below), the ROI calculus gets uncomfortable fast.

The pullback isn't uniform. Teams with tight eval loops and clear productivity signals are defending their spend. Teams that deployed broadly and measured loosely are getting squeezed. The companies that survive this round of budget scrutiny are the ones that instrumented everything from day one.

"In the past 2-3 months, spending on AI agents has exploded at many tech companies. Details from 15 of them, including the different ways they are coping with this realization."

— Gergely Orosz, The Pragmatic Engineer

46% of AI Pull Requests Get Rejected — Here's Why

A new paper studying the AIDev dataset of agent-generated pull requests landed this week with a number that should make anyone building coding agents stop and think: 46.41% of fixes proposed by Copilot, Devin, Cursor, and Claude are rejected. Nearly half. The researchers aren't just reporting the rate — they're diagnosing the failure modes, which is where it gets actionable.

The rejections aren't random. They cluster around a few recurring patterns: agents that technically fix the reported bug but break adjacent behavior, changes that don't match the codebase's conventions even when syntactically correct, and patches that solve a symptom rather than the root cause. These are exactly the failure modes you'd expect from agents that optimize for "make the test pass" rather than "understand what the code is supposed to do."

The wasted resources dimension is real. Every rejected PR means a human read it, ran CI, left review comments, and ultimately discarded the work. At scale across hundreds of engineers, that's not a rounding error — it's a tax on the entire engineering organization's attention. The paper frames this as a supervision and verification problem, which aligns with the broader push toward better evals for coding agents that Hamel Husain has been documenting in his evals-skills toolkit built from patterns across 50+ companies.

"46.41% of the fixes proposed by the agents Copilot, Devin, Cursor, and Claude are rejected. This represents a significant amount of wasted resources that require human reviews, verifications, and running tests and validations for fixes that are merely discarded."

— Abujadallah et al., arxiv:2606.13468

Google DeepMind Puts $10M on Multi-Agent Safety

Google DeepMind and partners announced a $10 million funding call specifically targeting multi-agent AI safety research — a meaningful signal that the field has moved from "agents are a demo" to "agents are infrastructure we need to understand how to not break." The timing isn't coincidental. With multi-agent systems now running in production at scale, the failure modes have shifted from theoretical to operational.

The funding call is broad — covering coordination failures, emergent behavior in agent swarms, and what happens when agents with different objectives negotiate shared resources — but the emphasis on "safety" here isn't the existential kind. It's closer to reliability engineering: how do you build systems of agents that fail gracefully, audit their own decisions, and don't amplify small errors into cascading failures?

This connects directly to a concurrent arxiv paper on Orchestration Reward Modeling (OrchRM), which proposes a self-supervised framework for evaluating orchestration quality in multi-agent systems without requiring human annotations. The core idea: use intermediate artifacts from agent executions to construct win-lose pairs, then train a Bradley-Terry reward model. It's a pragmatic workaround for the annotation bottleneck that makes supervised training of orchestrators so expensive.

Together, these two developments — DeepMind's funding call and the OrchRM paper — sketch the research agenda for the next 18 months: less "can agents do the task" and more "can we reliably verify that agents did it correctly."

"OrchRM leverages intermediate artifacts from multi-agent executions to construct win-lose pairs for Bradley-Terry reward model training."

— Tsang et al., arxiv:2606.13598

EvoArena Asks: What Happens When the Environment Changes Under an Agent?

Most agent benchmarks assume a static world. Deploy the agent, run the eval, measure success. EvoArena takes a different angle: environments change over time — software versions update, terminal behavior shifts, task conditions evolve — and agents that were performant yesterday fail today because their internal model of the world is stale.

The benchmark models these changes as sequences of progressive updates across terminal, software, and socially-mediated environments. It's a direct challenge to the way most teams currently validate agents: a one-shot eval against a frozen environment tells you almost nothing about how the agent will behave six months into deployment when the APIs it calls have changed, the codebase it works in has grown, and the context it was given at setup is no longer accurate.

This connects to a theme Eugene Yan has been developing — that context is infrastructure, not a one-time prompt. If context is infrastructure, then keeping that infrastructure current as the environment evolves is a maintenance burden most teams haven't budgeted for. EvoArena at least gives you a way to measure the degradation. Whether teams actually run continuous evals against evolving environments is a different question — most don't, which is part of why the 46% PR rejection rate above persists even as agent models improve.

"Real-world deployment is inherently dynamic, requiring agents to continually align their knowledge, skills, and behavior with changing environments and updated task conditions."

— Xu et al., EvoArena, arxiv:2606.13681

How China's Open AI Ecosystem Compounds — and Why It Matters

Nathan Lambert's analysis of how China's open model ecosystem compounds — published alongside his firsthand notes from visiting Chinese AI labs — is worth sitting with. The argument isn't that Chinese labs are winning on benchmarks (though DeepSeek V4 has done that). It's structural: China's AI development is characterized by high participation and open-first culture, which creates a compounding dynamic where improvements propagate fast, fine-tuning work is shared, and the cost of catching up to a new baseline drops continuously.

The contrast with US labs is instructive. Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google are building around closed weights with API access — which generates revenue but limits the ecosystem flywheel. DeepSeek releasing V4 openly means every Chinese startup, university lab, and developer community can immediately build on top of it, find its failure modes, and publish improvements. That's a different innovation cadence than "wait for the next API update."

For builders outside China, the operational question is whether to build on closed models (better performance today, pricing and policy risk tomorrow) or open weights (more control, more maintenance). Lambert's framing suggests the open ecosystem is getting more competitive, not less, which changes the risk profile of the closed-model-only bet.

"Further reflections on China's high-participation, open-first AI ecosystem."

— Nathan Lambert, Interconnects

What This All Means

  1. The AI spend pullback is real and arriving at exactly the wrong time for teams that deployed broadly without instrumentation. If you can't show cost-per-outcome metrics by end of Q3, your AI tooling budget is a target — start tracking merged PRs per dollar, not just seats provisioned.
  2. A 46% PR rejection rate across the four leading coding agents means the productivity narrative requires qualification. If you're pitching coding agents internally, model the review overhead and rejection cost alongside the generation speed — the net gain is real but smaller than the headline numbers suggest.
  3. DeepMind's $10M safety call and the OrchRM paper together signal that orchestration verification is becoming a funded research priority. Teams building multi-agent pipelines should be designing for auditability now — self-supervised reward models on execution artifacts are a near-term path to catching orchestration failures without expensive human annotation.
  4. EvoArena's dynamic environment framing should change how you scope agent evals. A benchmark run once at deployment is a snapshot, not a signal. Build continuous re-evaluation against updated environments into your infra the same way you'd build regression tests — because the agent's world keeps changing whether you measure it or not.
