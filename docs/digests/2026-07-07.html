Memory Wars, Realtime, Self-Improvement

OpenAI quietly ships GPT-realtime-2.1 to the API, a wave of agent memory architectures challenges the RAG consensus, and Lilian Weng publishes her most ambitious post yet on recursive self-improvement — all on the same Monday.

GPT-Realtime-2.1 Lands on the API — and It's Not a Soft Update

OpenAI pushed two new models to the Realtime API early Monday: gpt-realtime-2.1 and gpt-realtime-2.1-mini. The announcement hit the developer forum with minimal fanfare — no blog post, no launch event — which has become OpenAI's pattern for infrastructure-tier upgrades that nonetheless shift what's possible in production.

The mini variant is the more interesting signal. A cost-optimized realtime model means OpenAI is now treating low-latency voice and multimodal streaming as a commodity tier, not a premium feature. Earlier this year the Realtime API was still something teams used experimentally; shipping a mini variant suggests real enough adoption to justify segmenting the market. Any product betting on sub-200ms voice interaction — call automation, live transcription, voice-first agents — just got a cheaper inference path to evaluate.

The timing alongside Google's computer-use launch in Gemini 3.5 Flash (from late June) isn't coincidental. Both companies are racing to make real-time, multimodal, action-capable models cheap enough for high-volume consumer surfaces. Flash for vision+action, realtime-2.1-mini for voice — the middle of the model lineup is where the product wars are actually happening right now, not at the frontier.

"New realtime models on the API — gpt-realtime-2.1 and gpt-realtime-2.1-mini"

— OpenAI Community forum post, July 7 2026

Builders running on the previous realtime generation should benchmark 2.1-mini on latency and cost before assuming the full 2.1 is necessary — the mini tier may close most of the quality gap at a fraction of the inference spend.

The Agent Memory Problem Gets Serious Architecture Proposals

Two papers published this week make the same uncomfortable argument in different technical dialects: RAG-based memory is insufficient for agents that need to persist and reason across sessions, and the field needs structural replacements, not iterative patches.

MRMS (Multi-Resolution Memory Substrate) proposes organizing agent memory along two orthogonal axes — a representational axis spanning structured records, vectors, and graph relations, and a temporal axis that distinguishes working memory from episodic and long-term stores. The key claim is that a flat vector store collapses distinctions that agents actually need: "personal context" versus "external evidence," recent versus historical, mutable versus archival. Extending the context window doesn't solve this because retrieval precision, not window size, is the bottleneck.

MOSS (Memory-Orchestrated Semantic System) comes at the same problem from an auditability angle. The authors replace embedding-based retrieval with an agent that queries a structured relational database directly — meaning every retrieval step is SQL-readable, debuggable, and correctable. Their argument against RAG is blunt: it's "opaque by construction, difficult to audit, and bounded by the theoretical limits of vector representations."

"Long-lived AI agents require continuity across interactions, but continuity cannot be obtained by simply extending the prompt window."

— MRMS paper, Jizhizi Li & Amy Shi-Nash, arXiv 2607.04617

These aren't incremental refinements — they're competing memory paradigms. If either gains traction in production systems, it changes the architecture layer where most RAG engineering currently lives. The MOSS auditability argument in particular maps directly onto enterprise compliance requirements that vector stores have always struggled to satisfy.

Natural Language Tools Beat Structured Calling — Across 14 Models

A replication study published on arXiv this weekend validated one of the more counterintuitive findings in recent agent research: giving LLMs their tools described in natural language, rather than as structured JSON schemas, produces 14.9 percentage points better tool-calling accuracy on average — 62.3% versus 47.4% — across 8,560 trials spanning 14 models including frontier, reasoning, and open-weight variants.

The original Johnson et al. (2025) finding was already provocative. This replication extends it to newer models and holds. The intuition: models are trained overwhelmingly on natural language, so natural language tool descriptions activate the same representations as the rest of the generation task. Structured schemas, by contrast, require a format-switching cognitive mode that introduces systematic errors — especially on edge cases and ambiguous inputs.

For teams currently investing in elaborate JSON schema engineering for function calling, this is worth a direct empirical test on your own task distribution. The gains won't be uniform — tool complexity, model size, and domain all modulate the effect — but a 15-point accuracy lift is not a rounding error. The cost of running your own 50-trial A/B is essentially zero.

"NLT improves tool-calling accuracy by 14.9 percentage points overall (62.3% versus 47.4% structured) ... The results confirm the core findings and add detail."

— Somma, Plante & Premji, arXiv 2607.03953

The uncomfortable flip side: if natural language tool definitions outperform structured schemas, the entire ecosystem of schema-first tool registries and MCP-style protocol layers is optimizing for developer ergonomics, not model performance. Those aren't the same thing.

Lilian Weng on Recursive Self-Improvement — The Research Landscape, Mapped

On July 4th, Lilian Weng published what may be her most ambitious Lil'Log post in years: "Harness Engineering for Self-Improvement", a comprehensive survey of how modern systems can be built to improve themselves. The framing is deliberately engineering-first — not the philosophical RSI speculation of Yudkowsky (whom she cites) but a practical taxonomy of the mechanisms researchers are actually building today.

The post maps four categories of self-improvement harnesses: data flywheel systems where model outputs become training inputs; reward model bootstrapping; code generation loops where models write and run their own evaluators; and architecture search where models participate in their own design. The critical engineering insight Weng surfaces is the stability condition: self-improvement loops require careful harness design to avoid reward hacking and distribution collapse, the same failure modes she documented in detail in her reward hacking post from late 2024.

"The concept of recursive self-improvement (RSI) dates back to I. J. Good (1965)... The unprecedented capabilities of foundation models have made it finally possible to develop autonomous systems that improve through cycles of generation, evaluation, and refinement."

— Lilian Weng, "Harness Engineering for Self-Improvement," Lil'Log, July 2026

What makes this post land differently from the usual RSI discourse is that Weng grounds every mechanism in existing, working systems — RLHF variants, Constitutional AI, AlphaCode-style generation-and-test loops — rather than hypothetical future capabilities. This is research-to-production translation in real time. Teams building evaluation pipelines and training loops should read it as a threat model for their own systems as much as an inspiration document.

AgentGym2 Measures Agents Where They Actually Fail

Most agent benchmarks test what researchers wish agents could do. AgentGym2, published Monday, tests what they actually encounter: noisy inputs, missing specifications, incomplete tool interfaces, and tasks that require working through ambiguity rather than executing a clean plan. The benchmark is explicitly "de-idealized" — the authors' direct critique of existing evaluations that "rely on pre-packaged tool interfaces, overlook critical steps, and assume inputs are clean and fully specified."

The gap between idealized and de-idealized performance is the number that matters for anyone shipping agents into real pipelines. A model that scores well on existing benchmarks while assuming clean, fully-specified inputs is a model that will fail in the first week of production when users submit malformed requests, tools return unexpected errors, or tasks decompose differently than anticipated.

AgentGym2 joins a growing cluster of "production-realistic" evals — CRUX from AI Snake Oil earlier this year, ScarfBench for code migration — that are trying to close the gap between leaderboard scores and real-world utility. The common thread: length, mess, and underspecification are the variables that matter, and they're absent from most standard suites.

"Most existing benchmarks evaluate agents in simplified, idealized settings... Consequently, they understate the difficulty of real deployments, where uncertainty and noise are unavoidable."

— Zhiheng Xi et al., AgentGym2, arXiv 2607.05174

The LLMs-encode-remaining-output-length finding from a parallel paper — that 7-8B models carry a linear internal estimate of how much response they have left — offers a different angle on agent reliability: if models know their own length budget, you can probe that representation to catch runaway generation before it ships tokens.

What This All Means

  1. OpenAI's gpt-realtime-2.1-mini is the first signal that real-time voice inference is being commoditized — if your product uses the previous realtime generation, benchmark the mini variant this week; the cost reduction may exceed the quality tradeoff significantly for most use cases.
  2. The NLT replication study (14.9-point accuracy gain across 14 models) is direct evidence that natural language tool descriptions outperform structured JSON schemas — before investing further in schema tooling or protocol layers, run a 50-trial head-to-head on your own task distribution.
  3. The MRMS and MOSS papers represent serious architectural alternatives to flat vector RAG for long-lived agents; if you're building anything that needs to persist memory across sessions and satisfy audit requirements, these papers define the design space worth evaluating now, not after your first production incident.
  4. AgentGym2's "de-idealized" framing should change how you interpret your own evals: if your benchmark assumes clean inputs and pre-packaged tools, your production failure rate is almost certainly higher than your test scores suggest — add noise, ambiguity, and partial specifications to your eval harness deliberately.
