Google's Agentic Era, Codex Goes Enterprise

Google I/O 2026 declared the 'agentic Gemini era' while OpenAI quietly pushed Codex into Fortune 500 infrastructure. Meanwhile, the open-weight model bonanza accelerates and a SpaceX S-1 reveals Anthropic's surprising cloud bet.

Google I/O: 100 Announcements, One Clear Bet

Google didn't hold back at I/O 2026 — 100 announcements, with Sundar Pichai framing the entire moment as "the agentic Gemini era." The headline model drop was Gemini 3.5 Flash, which went straight to general availability — no preview modifier — and is already powering Google's own consumer products at scale, including AI Mode in Search and the Gemini app for billions of users.

For builders, the signal is clear: Gemini 3.5 Flash is Google's workhorse, not a showcase model. Simon Willison noted it's more expensive than its predecessor but Google is betting on it everywhere. The new Gemini Spark (background agents) and Antigravity 2.0 fill out an agent infrastructure story that's converging hard with what Anthropic and OpenAI have been building. Workspace got deep AI integration updates, and AI Mode in Search is reshaping how people find information — which has massive downstream implications for any product that currently relies on organic search traffic.

The honest caveat from Willison: "I have a policy of not writing about anything that I can't try out myself, and a lot of the big announcements are 'coming soon'." That gap between announced and shipped is still Google's Achilles heel, but the GA on Gemini 3.5 Flash is a real, usable signal today.

"3.5 Flash is available today to billions of people globally: For everyone via the Gemini app and AI Mode in Search."

Codex Goes Deep Enterprise — and Gets Real Users

OpenAI's Codex push is no longer a developer preview story. This week brought two major enterprise moves: a partnership with Dell to bring Codex into hybrid and on-premise environments, directly targeting regulated industries that can't send code to public APIs. And a detailed case study from Ramp showing engineers using Codex with GPT-5.5 to get substantive code review feedback in minutes instead of hours.

The Databricks angle is equally telling: GPT-5.5 is now powering Databricks enterprise agent workflows after setting a new state-of-the-art on OfficeQA Pro. These aren't fluffy announcements — they're integration points where AI coding assistance meets data pipelines and enterprise compliance requirements. The Dell deal in particular signals OpenAI is serious about the air-gapped, on-prem segment that has largely resisted cloud AI adoption.

SpaceX x Anthropic and the Compute Deals Under the Hood

The most interesting strategic disclosure of the week came buried in a SpaceX S-1 filing. Simon Willison flagged it: SpaceX's Colossus II data center — the same facility training Grok 5 — has signed Cloud Services Agreements with Anthropic. That means Anthropic is renting compute from Elon Musk's infrastructure operation while simultaneously competing with xAI, the company also training on that hardware.

The implications are layered. First, it confirms that frontier compute is genuinely scarce enough that even well-funded labs like Anthropic are buying capacity wherever they can find it, regardless of competitive optics. Second, it shows that the "infrastructure layer" of AI is becoming a strategic battleground distinct from the model layer — whoever controls the GPUs controls leverage over everyone building on top. Third, for builders thinking about vendor risk in their AI stack: the supply chains powering your API calls are more intertwined and fragile than the clean product interfaces suggest.

This comes in the same week Railway — the cloud platform — was profiled as an "agent-native cloud" seeing $200K+ in monthly coding agent spend, 3M users, and 100K signups per week. The infrastructure layer is being rebuilt from scratch with agents as the primary tenant, not humans. The question isn't if you need to think about agent infrastructure — it's whether you're already behind.

"In May 2026, we entered into Cloud Services Agreements with Anthropic PBC, an AI research and development company."

— SpaceX S-1 filing, via Simon Willison

Open-Weight Explosion: From Gemma 4 to DeepSeek V4

The open-weight model ecosystem just had its most crowded month ever. Interconnects catalogued the bonanza: Gemma 4, DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, MiMo 2.5, GLM-5.1, and others all dropped within weeks of each other. Sebastian Raschka has been tracking the architectural innovations underneath — KV sharing, multi-head compression, and compressed attention are the techniques that are actually making long-context inference economically viable in open models.

Nathan Lambert's analysis of how open model ecosystems compound is worth reading in full for PM strategy: China's open-first, high-participation AI ecosystem is producing a flywheel effect where each release builds on previous ones in ways that closed ecosystems structurally can't match. His earlier notes from visiting Chinese AI labs add texture — the participation density is qualitatively different from the West's more siloed lab structure.

For builders, the practical question is: at what point does an open-weight model at your price point match the closed API for your use case? That crossover point is arriving faster than most product roadmaps have planned for. The DeepSeek-as-Claude-Code alternative experiment on HN, while low-engagement, is a canary — engineers are actively trying to replicate closed-tool workflows on open weights.

"It's not benchmark scores" that make an open model succeed. Hint: it's ecosystem fit, tooling, and community velocity.

— Nathan Lambert, Interconnects, on what makes open models win

KV Cache, Token Speed, and the Hidden Cost of Long Context

Two pieces of infrastructure writing today converge on a problem that every team scaling RAG or agentic pipelines hits: memory costs are non-obvious and they bite you late. A well-timed explainer on KV cache surfaced on HN, breaking down exactly why serving long-context LLMs is so expensive — the key-value cache that enables fast autoregressive generation scales quadratically with context length and linearly with batch size, eating GPU memory that could otherwise handle more concurrent users.

This connects directly to Sebastian Raschka's architectural coverage of KV sharing and compressed attention in Gemma 4 and DeepSeek V4 — the reason these architectural choices matter for builders isn't academic. They directly determine whether 128K-context inference is economically viable in your product at scale, or whether you're bleeding margin every time a user pastes a long document.

Simon Willison also shared a useful interactive tool for visualizing token output speeds — Mike Veerman's HTML app lets you feel the difference between 10 tokens/second and 800 tokens/second. This matters for UX decisions: latency perception is non-linear, and what feels "fast enough" for a text editor copilot is completely different from what's tolerable in a voice interface or agentic loop. If you're designing streaming UI and haven't calibrated your team's intuition on actual token speeds, this tool is worth five minutes.

"The extremely high inference cost, in both time and memory, is a big bottleneck for adopting a powerful transformer for solving real-world tasks at scale."

— Lilian Weng, Lil'Log, Large Transformer Model Inference Optimization
