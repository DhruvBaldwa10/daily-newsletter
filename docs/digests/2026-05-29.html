Anthropic Hits $47B, Agents Take Over

Anthropic's Series H and $47B run-rate revenue signals the PMF moment is real — not projected. Meanwhile, Codex is eating enterprise dev workflows, a mystery model tops OpenRouter, and Claude Code is getting a serious ecosystem of its own.

Anthropic's $47B Run-Rate Is a Reality Check for Everyone

The numbers coming out of Anthropic right now should recalibrate every AI product team's sense of what's possible — and what's at stake. Simon Willison flagged the key line buried in Anthropic's $65B Series H announcement: run-rate revenue crossed $47 billion earlier this month. That's not ARR — it's an annualized projection from current monthly revenue — but the trajectory is staggering, especially given the Series G was only in February.

Willison has been tracking this habit of Anthropic sharing run-rate figures in funding announcements, and the acceleration is hard to ignore. This comes alongside his earlier note that Anthropic is reportedly on the verge of its first profitable quarter, and stories are already circulating about companies getting surprise-sized LLM bills driven by Claude Code usage among engineering staff. The Information reported on Uber's CTO noting that Claude Code can "blow AI budgets" — which is simultaneously a warning and a signal of deep adoption.

What changed? Willison's thesis is simple: both OpenAI and Anthropic have found genuine product-market fit. Not the "interesting toy" PMF of 2023, but the kind where enterprise teams are building production workflows on top of these models and paying accordingly. For builders, this means the competitive pressure from well-funded, deeply embedded incumbents is intensifying fast. The window to differentiate on top of these platforms is real but narrowing.

"Stories are circulating of companies surprised at how expensive their LLM bills are becoming from usage by their staff. I think this is because OpenAI and Anthropic have both found product-market fit."

— Simon Willison

The Mystery Model on Top of OpenRouter: Hy3

A strange signal appeared this week that deserves more attention than it's getting. Max Woolf wrote up the Hy3 LLM, which is topping OpenRouter's model rankings by a substantial margin — and almost nobody knows who built it or where it came from. The HN thread is sparse (12 comments, 36 points), but the underlying story is interesting for anyone thinking about model selection and routing infrastructure.

OpenRouter has become a genuine benchmark for real-world model performance — not synthetic evals, but actual usage patterns across thousands of developers and applications. When an unknown model surfaces at the top of those rankings, it raises several questions that matter for product builders: Is this an internal model someone deployed for testing? A fine-tune of an existing base that's dramatically outperforming on specific task distributions? A signal that the leaderboard itself is gameable?

The timing is also notable. Fireworks and Baseten recently raised at decacorn valuations, and OpenRouter itself is reportedly on the way to its own major raise. Inference routing infrastructure is becoming a serious business, and the models flowing through these systems are increasingly diverse — open-weight, fine-tuned, mystery. For PMs building on top of model APIs, the implication is that your routing and eval strategy can't just track the major labs anymore. You need to be watching what's actually winning in the wild.

Agentic Security Is Broken and Getting Worse

Two data points this week paint a worrying picture for anyone shipping agentic products. First, Simon Willison covered a PromptArmor disclosure showing that Microsoft Copilot Cowork — a production enterprise product — could be manipulated to exfiltrate files. The attack vector is the now-familiar prompt injection via untrusted content in the agent's context. Willison's framing is direct: "The biggest challenge in designing agentic systems continues to be preventing them from enabling attackers to exfiltrate data."

Second, curl's lead developer Daniel Stenberg published a post called "The pressure" documenting an unprecedented surge in AI-assisted security reports — now more than one credible report per day, 4-5x the 2024 rate and double 2025. The key word is "credible" — these aren't spam reports, they're real findings accelerated by AI tooling. The security research surface is expanding faster than any team can patch.

For builders shipping agentic features, these two stories together represent a strategic forcing function. The Copilot Cowork vulnerability is a reminder that production-grade agentic systems need explicit data exfiltration prevention at the architecture level — not as an afterthought. The curl story signals that your dependencies are being audited by AI at scale, whether you're ready or not. The SQLite team's new AGENTS.md file — essentially a guidance document for AI agents being pointed at their codebase — is an interesting adaptation: open source projects are starting to manage agent behavior directly.

"The rate of incoming security reports is 4-5 times higher than it was in 2024 and double the speed of 2025 — meaning that on average we now get more than one report per day."

— Daniel Stenberg, curl lead developer, via Simon Willison

Open Models Are Compounding — And China Is Lapping the Field

The open model ecosystem story is getting more complex and more consequential. Nathan Lambert's May 2026 outlook piece touches on Gemini Flash 3.5, a model called Mythos, and the shifting open-closed balance. But the deeper thread is in his earlier writing: how open model ecosystems compound and notes from his trip inside China's AI labs. The summary: China's high-participation, open-first ecosystem is creating compounding effects that closed labs are structurally slower to match.

The latest open artifacts roundup catalogues a genuinely impressive month: Gemma 4, DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, MiMo 2.5, GLM-5.1, and others. Sebastian Raschka's architectural deep-dive explains how models like Gemma 4 and DeepSeek V4 are using KV sharing, multi-head compression, and compressed attention to dramatically reduce long-context inference costs — with real implications for what you can run at what price point.

For product builders, the open model surge isn't just a cost story. It's an architectural flexibility story. Fine-tuning on domain data, running locally for privacy-sensitive applications, deploying in air-gapped environments — all of these use cases are becoming more viable as open-weight frontier models improve. Lambert's analysis of the open-closed performance gap suggests the delta is shrinking faster than most enterprise buyers realize, which changes the procurement conversation significantly.

"An eventful month with one flagship release after another" — the open model bonanza of May 2026 includes Gemma 4, DeepSeek V4, Kimi K2.6, and more.

What This All Means

  1. Actionable insight. Budget conversations are changing — fast. Claude Code and Codex are generating real, surprise-level spend at enterprise scale. If you're building internal AI tooling or selling to enterprise teams, build cost visibility and budget guardrails into your product now, not as a V2 feature. The Uber story is a warning and an opportunity.
  2. Actionable insight. Audit your agentic surface for data exfiltration vectors before you ship. The Copilot Cowork vulnerability is a template attack, not a one-off. Every agentic system that reads external content (email, docs, web) and has write/send capabilities needs explicit sandboxing and exfiltration prevention — treat it as a security primitive, not a compliance checkbox.
  3. Actionable insight. Add real-world routing signals to your model evaluation stack. The Hy3 mystery on OpenRouter is a reminder that benchmark leaderboards lag actual production performance. If you're making model selection or routing decisions, instrument your own usage data and cross-reference with OpenRouter rankings alongside lab-published evals.
  4. Actionable insight. Revisit your open-weight model strategy for cost-sensitive or privacy-sensitive workloads. With Gemma 4, DeepSeek V4, and Kimi K2.6 all landing in a single month, the capability-per-dollar curve on open models is moving fast. If you've been defaulting to closed APIs for everything, a targeted fine-tune on a strong open-weight base may now be both cheaper and more controllable for your specific domain.
