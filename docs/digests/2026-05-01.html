Agents Get Goals, Goblins Explained

Codex sprouts persistent goal-loops, OpenAI explains its goblin regression, and the community wrestles with when to trust — and when to distrust — AI coding agents. A defining week for how builders think about autonomous code execution.

Codex Gets Persistent Goals — and a New Orchestration Spec

Two big moves from OpenAI this week signal a maturation in how coding agents are expected to operate. First, Codex CLI 0.128.0 shipped a /goal command — essentially their own take on the Ralph loop pattern — that lets the agent keep iterating autonomously until it either evaluates the goal as complete or burns through a configured token budget. This is a meaningful shift from "run this command" to "achieve this outcome," which changes how you architect human-in-the-loop checkpoints.

Second, OpenAI published Symphony, an open-source spec for Codex orchestration that turns issue trackers into always-on agent systems. The framing here is explicit: reduce engineering context-switching by letting agents pick up work from your backlog continuously. Combined with the news that OpenAI models, Codex, and Managed Agents are now available on AWS, the picture is clear — OpenAI is building toward a world where Codex isn't a CLI tool you invoke, it's infrastructure you run.

Meanwhile, HN users are already asking how to extend Codex sessions, which tells you something real: the token-budget ceiling is hitting in practice. If you're building on top of Codex, designing graceful handoff points before budget exhaustion isn't optional — it's table stakes UX.

"You can now set a /goal and Codex will keep on looping until it evaluates that the goal has been completed... or the configured token budget has been exhausted."

— Simon Willison, summarizing Codex CLI 0.128.0

The Goblin Post Is Required Reading for Every AI PM

OpenAI published a detailed post-mortem on the "goblin" outputs that caused GPT-5 to behave with strange, personality-driven quirks — and it's one of the more honest pieces of infrastructure writing the company has released. The short version: emergent behavioral artifacts from training leaked into production in ways that weren't caught by standard evals. The goblins weren't a single bug; they were a symptom of how hard it is to fully characterize model behavior before release.

For builders, this is the cautionary tale to bookmark. Eval suites catch what you think to test for. Personality regressions, tonal drift, and unexpected refusal patterns often slip through because nobody wrote a test for "does the model suddenly start making goblin references." Eugene Yan's canonical piece on task-specific evals is directly relevant here — the distinction between evals that work and evals that don't often comes down to whether you've modeled failure modes that feel silly until they happen in production.

There's also a deeper product lesson: user trust is fragile. One weird behavioral cluster — even if harmless — creates disproportionate confusion and support load. If you're shipping on top of foundation models, you need behavioral regression testing as a first-class concern, not an afterthought. The goblin episode is a reminder that your model vendor's training pipeline is part of your stack, whether you like it or not.

Claude Code Failure Modes and the "Digital Smell" of AI-Generated Work

A post making the rounds on HN this week — "Claude Code is going to fail you eventually, and you need to be ready" — captures the operational reality that many builders are now living with: agentic coding tools are powerful enough to be genuinely useful, but not reliable enough to run unmonitored. The failure modes aren't random; they're patterned. Understanding them is what separates teams that ship with AI from teams that clean up after it.

This connects directly to a striking thread Simon Willison highlighted from Zig's Andrew Kelley, who has one of the strictest anti-LLM contribution policies in open source. Kelley's claim is worth sitting with: "people who come from the world of agentic coding have a certain digital smell that is not obvious to them but is obvious to us." This isn't just gatekeeping — it's a signal about code quality and reasoning depth that experienced maintainers claim they can reliably detect.

For product builders, the implication cuts both ways. You can ship faster with AI coding agents. But if your codebase is the product, or if it needs to be maintained by humans who care deeply about craft, the social and technical debt of AI-generated code is real and under-discussed. Sebastian Raschka's recent deep-dive on the components of a coding agent — tools, memory, and repo context — gives a useful mental model for where these failure modes originate.

"People who come from the world of agentic coding have a certain digital smell that is not obvious to them but is obvious to us."

— Andrew Kelley, Zig project lead, via Simon Willison

KV Cache Locality: The Inference Cost Variable Nobody Talks About

A quietly important post surfaced on HN: "KV Cache Locality: The Hidden Variable in Your LLM Serving Cost." As teams move from prototypes to production workloads, inference cost optimization is becoming a real engineering discipline — and KV cache behavior is one of the least-understood levers. The core insight: cache hit rates vary dramatically based on how you structure and route requests, and the delta between good and bad cache locality can represent a 2-5x difference in effective cost per token at scale.

This matters more as the industry shifts what Latent Space is calling "The Inference Inflection" — a moment where inference spend is beginning to dominate training spend for many organizations, and where the economics of serving at scale demand the same rigor previously reserved for training pipelines. Google's simultaneous announcement of two new specialized TPUs for the agentic era — distinct chips for training and inference workloads — underlines that the infrastructure layer is bifurcating to serve these different cost profiles.

For builders who are still treating inference as a commodity API call: the era of "just throw it at the endpoint" is ending. Teams that understand prefix caching, request batching, and prompt structure's impact on cache locality will have a structural cost advantage. This is now a product strategy question, not just a DevOps one.

"A quiet day lets us reflect on the growing implications of the inference age."

— Latent Space (swyx), "The Inference Inflection"

GPT-5.5 Cyber Capabilities and the New Security Eval Landscape

The UK's AI Security Institute published its evaluation of GPT-5.5's cyber capabilities, finding it comparable to Claude Mythos in its ability to find security vulnerabilities — with the key difference that GPT-5.5 is generally available right now, while Mythos remains behind restricted access. This is a significant data point: frontier-level offensive security capability is now in broadly accessible models.

OpenAI simultaneously published a five-part cybersecurity action plan and introduced Advanced Account Security with phishing-resistant login. The juxtaposition is notable — as the models get better at finding vulnerabilities, the platforms hosting them have to harden their own attack surfaces. For AI PMs building anything in the security or compliance space, these evaluations are now your competitive landscape documents.

The broader implication for product builders: if you're shipping an AI product that operates with any degree of autonomy — browsing the web, executing code, calling APIs — your threat model needs to account for prompt injection and adversarial inputs at a level most product teams haven't gotten to yet. Lilian Weng's foundational work on adversarial attacks on LLMs remains the best primer, and the GPT-5.5 eval results suggest these attacks are becoming more tractable, not less.

— OpenAI Blog, "Cybersecurity in the Intelligence Age"
